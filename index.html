Berat is a city on the Osum River in central Albania. It's known for its white Ottoman houses. On a hilltop, Berat Castle is a huge compound still inhabited by townspeople. Within its walls are Byzantine churches, the Red Mosque, and the Onufri National Museum, which features Christian icons.

To the east stands the Ethnographic Museum, housed in an 18th-century building, showcasing traditional crafts and a reconstructed medieval bazaar.

Berat (pronounced [bɛˈɾat]; Albanian definite form: Berati) is the ninth most populous city in Albania and serves as the administrative center of both Berat County and Berat Municipality. It is located 71 kilometers (44 miles) north of Gjirokastër, 70 kilometers (43 miles) west of Korçë, 70 kilometers (43 miles) south of Tirana, and 33 kilometers (21 miles) east of Fier.

Situated in southern Albania, Berat is surrounded by mountains and hills, with Mount Tomorr to the east—declared a national park. The Osum River, stretching 161 kilometers (100 miles), flows through the city before joining the Seman River in the fertile Myzeqe Plain.

The municipality of Berat was formed in the 2015 local government reform, merging the former municipalities of Berat, Otllak, Roshnik, Sinjë, and Velabisht. As of the 2023 census, it has a population of 62,232 and covers an area of 421.6 square kilometers (162.8 square miles).

History of Berat

Berat, often referred to as the “City of a Thousand Windows,” boasts a continuous history dating back over 2,400 years. Once a Thracian settlement, it became part of the Roman and Byzantine Empires before experiencing Ottoman rule, leaving behind a unique architectural and cultural blend.

Culture & Heritage

Berat is a melting pot of Christian and Muslim traditions, seen in its architecture, religious sites, and annual festivals like the Tomorr Mountain pilgrimage. Locals are proud of their hospitality, artisan crafts, and culinary specialties such as 'qifqi' (rice balls) and sweet gliko preserves.
